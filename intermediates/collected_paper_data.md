## ABSTRACT

Chronic itch remains a highly prevalent disorder with limited treatment options. Most chronic itch diseases are thought to be driven by both the nervous and immune systems, but the fundamental molecular and cellular interactions that trigger the development of itch and the acute-to-chronic itch transition remain unknown. Here, we show that skin-infiltrating neutrophils are key initiators of itch in atopic dermatitis, the most prevalent chronic itch disorder. Neutrophil depletion significantly attenuated itch-evoked scratching in a mouse model of atopic dermatitis. Neutrophils were also required for several key hallmarks of chronic itch, including skin hyperinnervation, enhanced expression of itch signaling molecules, and upregulation of inflammatory cytokines, activity-induced genes, and markers of neuropathic itch. Finally, we demonstrate that neutrophils are required for induction of CXCL10, a ligand of the CXCR3 receptor that promotes itch via activation of sensory neurons, and we find that that CXCR3 antagonism attenuates chronic itch.

## INTRODUCTION

Chronic itch is a debilitating disorder that affects millions of people worldwide (Matterne et al., 2011; Mollanazar et al., 2016; Dalgard et al., 2015). It is a symptom of a number of skin diseases and systemic disorders, as well as a side effect of a growing list of medications. Like chronic pain, chronic itch can be a disease in and of itself (Stander and Steinhoff, 2002; Oaklander, 2011; Dhand and Aminoff, 2014). Unlike acute itch, which can facilitate removal of crawling insects, parasites, or irritants, persistent scratching in chronic itch disorders has no discernable benefit; scratching damages skin, leading to secondary infection, disfiguring lesions, and exacerbation of disease severity (Mollanazar et al., 2016; Yosipovitch and Papoiu, 2008; Ikoma et al., 2006). The most common chronic itch disorder is atopic dermatitis (AD; commonly known as eczema), which affects fifteen million people in the United States alone (Spergel and Paller, 2003). Severe AD can trigger the atopic march, where chronic itch and inflammation progress to food allergy, allergic rhinitis, and asthma (Spergel and Paller, 2003; Zheng et al., 2011).

Little is known about the underlying mechanisms that drive chronic itch pathogenesis. A number of studies have identified biomarkers and disease genes in itchy human AD lesions (Ewald et al., 2017; Choy et al., 2012; Guttman-Yassky et al., 2009; Suarez-Farinas et al., 2013; Jabbari et al., 2012). A recent study compared the transcriptomes of healthy skin to itchy and non-itchy skin from psoriasis and AD patients, revealing dramatic changes in expression of genes associated with cytokines, immune cells, epithelial cells, and sensory neurons (Nattkemper et al., 2018). However, due to the difficulty in staging lesion development and obtaining staged samples from patients, there is currently no temporal map of when individual molecules and cell types contribute to chronic itch pathogenesis.

We employed the MC903 mouse model of AD and the atopic march (Dai et al., 2017; Li et al., 2009; Li et al., 2006; Zhang et al., 2009; Moosbrugger-Martinz et al., 2017) to provide a framework within which to identify the molecules and cells that initiate the development of atopic itch. The MC903 model is ideal because of its highly reproducible phenotypes that closely resemble human AD. We performed RNA-seq of skin at key time points in the model. The primary goal of our study was to identify the inciting molecules and cell types driving development of chronic itch. We show that infiltration of neutrophils into skin is required for development of chronic itch. Additionally, we demonstrate that neutrophils direct early hyperinnervation of skin, and the upregulation of itch signaling molecules and activity-induced genes in sensory neurons. Finally, we identify CXCL10/CXCR3 signaling as a key link between infiltrating neutrophils and sensory neurons that drives itch behaviors.

## RESULTS

### MC903 triggers rapid changes in expression of skin barrier, epithelial cell-derived cytokine, and axon guidance genes

Using RNA-seq of MC903-treated skin, we observed distinct temporal patterns by which these classes of genes are differentially expressed across the first eight days of the model. Overall, we found that 62% of genes from a recent study of human chronic itch lesions (Nattkemper et al., 2018) and 67% of AD-related genes were significantly changed for at least one of the time points examined.

MC903 dramatically alters the transcriptional profile of keratinocytes. Proteases (Klk6, Klk13, among others) and skin barrier genes (Cdhr1) changed as early as six hours after the first treatment, before mice begin scratching. Beginning at day two, before any significant itch-evoked scratching, we saw increases in Tslp, as well as several other epithelial-derived cytokines, including the neutrophil chemoattractant genes Cxcl1, Cxcl2, Cxcl3, and Cxcl5. To ask whether upregulation of these chemokine genes was dependent on protease activity, we treated human keratinocytes with the protease-activated receptor two agonist SLIGRL. SLIGRL treatment triggered increased expression of several of these chemokine genes, including IL8 (the human ortholog of mouse Cxcl1/Cxcl2) and CXCL2.

In the skin we observed early changes in a number of transcripts encoding neuronal outgrowth factors (Ngf, Artn) and axon pathfinding molecules (Slit1, Sema3d, Sema3a), prior to when mice began scratching. We saw increased innervation of lesions at day two but not day one of the model. We saw an increase in CGRP+ innervation of skin at day 2, which suggests that elevation of neuronal transcripts in skin is due to hyperinnervation of peptidergic itch and/or pain fibers.

### Neutrophils are the first immune cells to infiltrate AD skin

By day five, mice exhibited robust itch behaviors and stark changes in a number of AD disease genes. For example, Flg2 levels sharply decreased at day five. Using flow cytometry, we observed that neutrophils were the most abundant immune cell subtype at day 5. It was not until day eight that we observed the classical AD-associated immune signature in the skin, with upregulation of Il4, Il33 and other Th2-associated genes. We also observed increases in the T cell chemoattractant genes Cxcl9, Cxcl10, and Cxcl11. Neutrophils and a number of other immune cells that started to infiltrate on day five were robustly elevated in skin by day eight, including basophils, CD4+ T cells, eosinophils, and mast cells, but not inflammatory monocytes.

CD4+ T cells are ubiquitous in mature human AD lesions and promote chronic AD itch and inflammation. We were quite surprised to find that itch behaviors preceded significant CD4+ T cell infiltration. Therefore, neutrophils drew our attention as potential early mediators of MC903 itch. Indeed, by mass spectrometry, we observed increases in several inflammatory lipids, PGD2 and PGE2, as well as LTB4 and its precursor 5-HETE in MC903-treated skin, implicating neutrophils in driving AD itch and inflammation.

### Neutrophils are required for early itch behaviors in the MC903 model of AD

Systemic depletion of neutrophils using daily injections of an anti-Gr1 (aGr1) antibody dramatically attenuated itch-evoked scratching through the first eight days of the model. Our depletion strategy significantly and selectively reduced circulating and skin-infiltrating neutrophils on days five and eight. In contrast, basophils and CD4+ T cells continued to infiltrate the skin following aGr1 treatment.

CXCL1 injection triggered robust scratching behaviors. Acute depletion of neutrophils with aGr1 resulted in a dramatic loss of CXCL1-evoked itch behaviors. This effect was specific to neutrophil-induced itch, as injection of chloroquine, a pruritogen that directly activates pruriceptors, still triggered robust scratching in aGr1-treated animals.

MC903-treated TSLPR KO mice displayed robust scratching behaviors through the first eight days of the model. TSLPR KO mice displayed robust neutrophil infiltration, but completely lacked basophil and CD4+ T cell infiltration into the skin. Neutrophil depletion in the second week with aGr1 robustly decreased skin-infiltrating neutrophils and substantially reduced scratching behaviors at day 12 (79% mean reduction in time spent scratching). Loss of TSLPR effected a 44% reduction in time spent scratching.

### MC903 drives rapid and robust changes in the peripheral and central nervous systems

We did not see any significant transcriptional changes in the trigeminal ganglia (TG) until five days after the first treatment, and in total only 84 genes were differentially expressed through the eighth day. These hits included genes related to excitability of itch sensory neurons, neuroinflammatory genes, and activity-induced or immediate early genes. We observed enrichment of neuronal markers expressed by 'NP3' itch neurons (Il31ra, Osmr, Trpa1, Cysltr2, and Nppb).

We used IHC to ask whether CD45+ immune cells increase in the TG. We observed a significant increase in TG immune cell counts at day eight but not day five. We performed RNA-seq on spinal cord segments that innervate the MC903-treated rostral back skin of mice. We saw upregulation of Lcn2 on day eight of the MC903 model and robust induction of immediate early genes (Fos, Junb), suggesting that MC903 itch drives activity-dependent changes in the spinal cord as early as one week after beginning treatment.

### Neutrophils are required for upregulation of select itch- and atopic-related genes, including the itch-inducing chemokine CXCL10

A number of AD-associated cytokines that were upregulated in control MC903 skin were not upregulated in TSLPR KO and neutrophil-depleted skin. For example, Il33 upregulation is both neutrophil- and TSLPR-dependent. By contrast, upregulation of epithelial-derived cytokines and chemokines Tslp, Cxcl1, Cxcl2, Cxcl3, and Cxcl5 was unaffected by either loss of TSLPR or neutrophil depletion.

Among hundreds of MC903-dependent genes examined, only a handful were uniquely affected by neutrophil depletion. One such gene was Cxcl10, a chemokine known to be released by skin epithelial cells, neutrophils, and other myeloid cells. Cxcl10 expression was increased in TSLPR KO but not neutrophil-depleted skin.

### CXCR3 signaling is necessary for MC903-evoked chronic itch

Neutrophil depletion attenuated the expression of activity-induced immediate early genes (Vgf, Junb) in the TG. Expression of Lcn2 and activity-induced genes Fos and Junb were not increased in spinal cord isolated from neutrophil-depleted animals. Neutrophil-depleted animals displayed no skin hyperinnervation at day two.

CXCL10 protein was increased in MC903-treated skin from uninjected wild-type and TSLPR KO animals, but not in skin from neutrophil-depleted mice. The CXCR3 antagonist AMG 487 strongly attenuated scratching behaviors on days five, eight, and twelve, with the greatest effect at day eight. CXCR3 blockade did not attenuate scratching behaviors in naive mice injected with chloroquine.

## DISCUSSION

The study demonstrates that neutrophils are essential for the early development of MC903-evoked itch. Neutrophil recruitment is sufficient to drive itch behaviors within minutes of infiltration. Neutrophils are recruited to the upper dermis and epidermis in AD and trigger itch. Neutrophil depletion in the second week of the MC903 model can attenuate chronic itch-evoked scratching.

The CXCR3 ligand CXCL10 contributes to mouse models of acute and allergic itch; however, its role in chronic itch was previously unknown. Induction of CXCL10 protein was completely dependent on neutrophils. The TSLP/IL-4 and neutrophil/CXCL10 pathways are not highly interdependent.

Drugs targeting neutrophils are currently in clinical trials for the treatment of psoriasis, asthma, and other inflammatory disorders. MDX-1100, a biologic that targets CXCL10, has already shown efficacy for treatment of rheumatoid arthritis in phase II clinical trials. CXCL10 or CXCR3 may be promising targets for treating chronic itch.

Overall, neutrophils incite itch and inflammation in early AD through: (1) directly triggering itch upon infiltration into the skin; and (2) indirectly triggering itch by altering expression of endogenous pruritogens (e.g., induction of Cxcl10 expression).

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

### Mouse Studies
All mice were housed in standard conditions in accordance with standards approved by the Animal Care and Use Committee of the University of California Berkeley (12 hr light-dark cycle, 21°C). Wild-type C57BL/6 mice were obtained from Charles River or Jackson Laboratories. TSLPR KO mice were kindly provided by Dr. Steven Ziegler (Crlf2tm1Jni; Carpino et al., 2004) and backcrossed onto C57BL/6.

### MC903 Model of Atopic Dermatitis
MC903 (Calcipotriol; R and D Systems) was applied to the shaved mouse cheek (20 mL of 0.2 mM in ethanol) or rostral back (40 mL of 0.2 mM in ethanol) once per day for 1–12 days. All MC903 studies were performed on 8–12 week old age-matched mice. Behavior, RNA-seq, flow cytometry, and immunohistochemistry were performed on days 1, 2, 3, 5, 8 and/or 12.

### MC903 RNA Isolation and Sequencing
On days 1 (six hours post-treatment), 2, 5, or 8 post-treatment, mice treated with MC903 and vehicle were euthanized via isoflurane and cervical dislocation. Cheek skin was removed, flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen, and cryo-homogenized. Ipsilateral trigeminal ganglia were dissected. Thoracic spinal cord was also collected. RNA was extracted using RNAzol RT. RNA pellets were DNase treated, resuspended in DEPC-treated water, and subjected to poly(A) selection and RNA-seq library preparation (Apollo 324) at the Functional Genomics Laboratory (UC Berkeley). Single-end read sequencing (length = 50 bp) was performed on an Illumina HiSeq4000. Data are available at Gene Expression Omnibus under GSE132173.

### MC903 RNA Sequencing Analysis
Reads were mapped to the mm10 mouse genome using Bowtie2 and Tophat. Reads were assigned to transcripts using htseq-count. For a given time point, replicate measurements were used as input for DESeq (R) and genes with padjusted <0.05 (skin and spinal cord) or padjusted <0.1 (trigeminal ganglia) for at least one time point were retained.

### Custom Gene Groups and Permutation Test
Genes were clustered into functional groups and significance was evaluated using a permutation test (10,000 iterations). For each gene group of n genes, the median absolute log2 fold change was calculated (ztrue). Then n random genes were drawn from all detected genes and the median log2 fold change was calculated (znull). The proportion of resampled gene groups exhibiting (ztrue >= znull) was taken as an empirical p-value.

### Flow Cytometry
Skin samples were collected with a 4- or 6 mm biopsy punch into cold RPMI. Skin samples were digested for 1 hr at 37°C using 1 U/mL Liberase TM and 5 mg/mL DNAse I. Cells were stained with LIVE/DEAD fixable stain Aqua, then blocked with anti-CD16/32 and stained with fluorophore-conjugated antibodies: cKit-Biotin (secondary: SA-FITC), CD11b-violet fluor 450, Ly6C-PerCP/Cy5.5, CD49b-PE/Cy7, CD45.2-APC/Cy7, FceRI-PE, Ly6G-AF700. 10 mL counting beads were added to measure absolute cell counts. Single cell suspensions were analyzed on an LSR II or LSR Fortessa (BD Biosciences), and data were analyzed using FlowJo (v.9.9.3).

### Human Keratinocyte RNA Sequencing
Normal human epidermal keratinocytes (PromoCell #C-12001) were cultured in PromoCell Keratinocyte Growth Medium 2. Cells were treated for three hours at room temperature with 100 mM SLIGRL or vehicle. Total RNA was extracted by column purification (Qiagen RNeasy Mini Kit). Sequences were trimmed (Trimmomatic), mapped (hg19, TopHat) and assigned to transcripts using htseq-count. Differential gene expression was assessed using R (edgeR). Data available at GEO under GSE132174.

### IHC of Whole-Mount Skin
Cheek skin was fixed overnight in 4% PFA, washed in PBS, and incubated in primary antibody (Rabbit anti beta-Tubulin III, Abcam #ab18207, or Rabbit anti-CGRP, Immunostar #24112) in blocking buffer for 6 days at 4°C. Secondary antibody (Goat anti-Rabbit Alexa 594) was applied for 3 days at 4°C. Skin was serially dried in methanol:PBS, cleared with BABB (benzyl alcohol:benzyl benzoate 1:2), and mounted. Whole-mount skin samples were imaged on a Zeiss LSM 880 confocal microscope with a 20x water objective. Image analysis was performed using a custom macro in FIJI.

### IHC of Sectioned Trigeminal Ganglia
TG were dissected, post-fixed in 4% PFA for 1 hr, cryo-protected in 30% sucrose-PBS, embedded in OCT, and cryosectioned at 14 mm. Primary antibodies: Mouse anti-CD45 (eBioscience #14-054-82) and Chicken anti-Peripherin (Abcam #39374). Secondary antibodies: Goat anti-Chicken Alexa 594 (ThermoFisher #A11042) and Goat anti-Mouse Alexa 488 (Abcam #150117). DAPI was used to mark nuclei. Imaging was performed on an Olympus IX71 microscope.

### Neutrophil Depletion
Neutrophils were acutely depleted using intraperitoneal injection with 250 mg aGR1 in PBS (clone RB6-8C5), 16–24 hr before behavioral and flow cytometry experiments. For longer depletion experiments, mice were injected beginning one day prior to MC903 administration and each afternoon thereafter through day 7 of the model.

### CXCL10 ELISA Measurements in Skin
On day 8, 6 mm biopsy punches of cheek skin were harvested, flash-frozen, cryo-homogenized, and homogenized on ice in tissue homogenization buffer (100 mM Tris pH 7.4, 150 mM NaCl, 1 mM EGTA, 1 mM EDTA, 1% Triton X-100, 0.5% Sodium deoxycholate). CXCL10 protein was quantified using the Mouse CXCL10 Duoset ELISA kit (R and D Systems; #DY466-05). Plate was read at 450 nm and CXCL10 was quantified using a seven-point standard curve with a 4-parameter logistic curve.

### Acute Itch Behavior
Mice were shaved one week prior to itch behavior and acclimated in behavior chambers for 30 min. Compounds injected: 1 mg carrier-free CXCL1 (R and D systems) in PBS, 3.31 mM AMG 487 (Tocris) in 20% HPCD-PBS, 50 mM Chloroquine diphosphate in PBS, plus vehicle controls. Acute pruritogens were injected using the cheek model (20 mL, s.c.). AMG 487 (50 mL) was injected s.c. into the rostral back skin one hour prior to recording.

### Lipidomics
Skin was homogenized in cold methanol to stabilize lipid mediators. Deuterated internal standards were added. LC-MS/MS system: Agilent 1200 Series HPLC, Luna C18 column (Phenomenex), and AB Sciex QTRAP 4500 mass spectrometer. Analysis was carried out in negative ion mode; lipid mediators quantified using scheduled multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) mode.

### DNFB Model of Atopic Dermatitis
Mice were sensitized with 25 mL 0.5% DNFB in 4:1 acetone:olive oil on the rostral back. Five days later, challenged with 40 mL 0.2% DNFB or vehicle on the outer surface of the right ear. Twenty-four hours after challenge, mice were euthanized and ear skin was harvested for flow cytometry.

### Statistical Analyses
Different control experimental conditions were pooled when the appropriate statistical test showed they were not significantly different (Supplementary file 2). Statistical tests used: Student's t-test, one-way ANOVA with Tukey-Kramer post hoc comparison, and two-way ANOVA with Tukey Kramer or Sidak's post-hoc comparison. Bar graphs show mean ± SEM. Statistical analyses performed using PRISM 7 software (GraphPad). Significance levels: *=0.01 < p < 0.05, **=0.001 < p < 0.01, ***=0.0001 < p < 0.001, ****= p < 0.0001.

## KEY FINDINGS SUMMARY

- Neutrophils are the first immune cells to infiltrate AD skin (peak at day 5)
- Neutrophil depletion with anti-Gr1 antibody dramatically attenuates scratching at days 5 and 8
- CXCL1-induced neutrophil recruitment is sufficient to trigger acute itch within minutes
- TSLPR KO mice scratch robustly but lack basophils and CD4+ T cells — supporting a neutrophil-specific early role
- Neutrophils are required for skin hyperinnervation at day 2
- Neutrophils are required for CXCL10 induction — CXCL10 is elevated in WT MC903 skin but not in neutrophil-depleted skin
- CXCR3 antagonist AMG 487 attenuates scratching at days 5, 8, and 12 but not chloroquine-induced acute itch
- Neuropathic gene Lcn2 is upregulated in spinal cord by day 8; absent in neutrophil-depleted mice
- Only 84 genes differentially expressed in trigeminal ganglia; enriched for NP3 itch neuron markers
